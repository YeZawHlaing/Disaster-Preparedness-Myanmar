Avalanche Survival Guide

Before

  • Keep track of weather before heading for snow-capped mountains.

During

  • Switch off the snowmobile engine.
  • Try to stay on the surface. You will have three times more chance of survival.
  • Push machinery, equipment, or heavy objects away from you to avoid injury.
  • Seek shelter (protector rocks or trees) and hold tight.
  • If you have found shelter, crouch facing away from the snow slide.
  • Cover your nose and mouth using a cloth to avoid suffocation.
  • Arch hands over your face to create an air space.
  • Try getting away from the avalanche path by moving to the side.
  • Try jerking towards the surface as a marker for rescuers.
  • If moving downward with the avalanche, stay on the surface using a swimming motion.

After

  • Once the avalanche stops, begin digging out quickly before the snow settles.
  • Mark the last seen location of team members using a cloth, pole, etc.
  • Do not smoke or use a lighter/matches as this consumes oxygen.
  • If available, leave a two-way radio on.

Treating the Affected

  • Free the head of the affected person first.
  • Remove snow and water from their mouth and nose.
  • Remove wet clothes and dry the affected person. Wrap them in dry clothes or blankets.
  • Administer CPR (Cardio Pulmonary Resuscitation) if needed, and give cardiac massage.
  • Seek medical attention immediately.

Avalanche Signs

  • Steep slopes between 25 and 45 degrees.
  • Convex (spoon-shaped) slopes are most dangerous between late December and the end of January.
  • North-facing slopes are more prone to avalanches in mid-winter, while south-facing slopes are dangerous in warmer spring conditions.
  • Smooth, grassy slopes are riskier than rocky or forested areas.
  • New snow increases avalanche risk.
